Pulmonary fibrosis (PF) is a complex and devastating disease2-4


Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF) and Progressive Pulmonary Fibrosis (PPF) are chronic, progressive interstitial lung diseases characterized by irreversible scarring of lung tissue.2-5



PPF is a complex and devastating disease2,3,11-13


PPF refers to a subset of non-IPF ILDs characterized by progression– worsening of respiratory symptoms, decline in FVC, and increased extent of fibrosis seen in HRCT scans.7,8


Patients with ILDs related to RA, SSc, and HP are at risk for developing PPF.9,10


The relentless and progressive nature of PPF can cause irreversible scarring and damage that will continue to deteriorate a patient s lung function and put them at risk for early mortality . 10,14-17


IPF is characterized by an aberrant wound-healing response18


Persistent injury induces inflammatory and fibrotic responses to repair damaged tissue.19


Abnormal wound healing in pulmonary fibrosis19



In IPF, F, the processes become uncontrolled , resulting in excessive fibrosis.20

IPF and PPF are deadly2



  • Depressive symptoms
  • Distress related to the inability to complete simple daily tasks



Disease progression is similar across IPF and PPF2,3


Disease progression is characterized by:2,3



Worsening respiratory symptoms



FVC or diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide (DL CO ) decline



Increasing extent of fibrosis on HRCT



FVC decline is considered a clinically significant indicators of progression. When that decline is 10% or greater, it also becomes an indicator of mortality risk for patients with IPF.4


Identifying lung function decline early is a top priority1


In IPF, lung function loss is continuous and cumulative.5



Stability today can turn into decline tomorrow as previous FVC decline cannot predict future FVC decline.8


Measures of lung function can provide important prognostic information1


FVC decline is a surrogate marker for mortality.9



DL co decline is associated with a poor prognosis9



Even a small decline in DL CO increases the risk of mortality or lung transplant in IPF.9

Disease management remains challenging


IPF and PPF are not only complex conditions to diagnose and manage but challenging to develop new treatments for as well. Over the past years, many drug development programs have faced substantial difficulty bringing new products through clinical trials to market. Pharmaceutical treatment5-7 Including antifibrotics (IPF/PPF), immunomodulators (PPF), and anti-inflammatories (PPF)

Interstitial lung disease


ILD is a common, often early, and potentially fatal manifestation of autoimmune diseases.1,2


Interstitial lung disease (ILD) is a common, often early, and potentially fatal manifestation of autoimmune diseases1,2


While autoimmune diseases can cause multi-organ damage, the lungs are particularly at risk, most often manifesting as an ILD.3




Due to the high prevalence of ILD in autoimmune diseases, it is important to screen your autoimmune patients to establish a baseline.7


Understanding AI-ILDs


Patients with AI-ILDs are at risk of developing pulmonary

( fibrosis (PF)8,9


Autoimmune interstitial lung disease (AI-ILD) involves fibrosis and inflammation of the lung, which is called pulmonary fibrosis.8,9



While AI ILDs differ, common pathogenic pathways to fibrogenesis are shared irrespective of the trigger for the lung injury.1,11,14-18


Patients with AI-ILDs are often asymptomatic or report nonspecific symptoms that can complicate diagnosis19


Patients may exhibit a wide range of symptoms , including:



Cough 20



Digital clubbing21



Fatigue 20



Dyspnea 20


Additionally, there are many diseases with similar signs and respiratory symptoms as PF, which can further complicate diagnosis.22


The heterogeneity of symptom manifestation in PF makes diagnosis and management inherently complex, so it is crucial to routinely screen your patients with Al-ILD . 18 , 19


Understanding progression


IPF and PPF are deadly.11



Up to 45% of patients with AI-ILDs can develop PPF23


PPF carries a heavy physical and emotional burden of patients, ultimately causing irreversible lung damage and early mortality.9


Pulmonary fibrosis is more deadly than many forms of cancer


5 year survival rates



Proactive , routine monitoring is crucial to promptly identify progression in AI ILD19


Progression is defined as the occurrence of at least 2 of the 3 criteria within 1 year with no alternative explanation.8


Worsening respiratory symptoms8


Physiological evidence of disease progression , defined as either8


  • Absolute decline in lung function of ≥5% predicted within 1 year of follow-up
  • Absolute decline in DL co (corrected for Hb) of ≥10% predicted within 1 year of follow-up

Radiological evidence of disease progression , defined as 1 or more of the following8


  • Increased extent or severity of traction bronchiectasis and bronchiolectasis
  • New ground-glass opacity with traction bronchiectasis
  • New fine reticulation
  • Increased extent or increased coarseness of reticular

abnormality


  • New or increased honeycombing
  • Increased lobar volume loss

Progression can have a devastating impact on quality of life, making it important to continuously monitor your patients with AI ILD.19,20


Management landscape


Expert guidance for the management of AI-ILDs


While ATS/ERS/JRS/ALAT guidelines for PPF are well established , evolving as needed over the years , the clinical guidance for diagnosis , monitoring , and management of patients with AI ILDs is relatively new and less widely understood.30
